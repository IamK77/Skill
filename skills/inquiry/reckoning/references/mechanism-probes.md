# Mechanism Probes & the Failure Boundary — proving WHY and where (STAGES 3-4)

This reference is the depth behind **STAGE 3 — Mechanism** and **STAGE 4 — Boundary** of the [../SKILL.md](../SKILL.md) pipeline. It governs the move from *the method works* to *the method works for the reason you claim, and stops working exactly where the reason runs out.* It backs three checks: `probes-not-identical-to-main-result` (the prediction is not the win itself), `two-probes-minimum` (the explanation stands on at least two independent probes), and `failure-edge-characterized` (the losing regime is found, written as a condition, and connected to the mechanism). Winning the main table is the entry fee, not the finding; everything here is what separates a publishable paper from a strong one. The single fact these two stages hang on:

> From the mechanism explanation M, derive a checkable prediction P that is NOT the main result itself — because "the method is better" cannot be evidence that "the mechanism holds", since the win is precisely what M was invoked to explain.

---

## The core action — a prediction that is not the win

You have an explanation M for why your method wins: *it exploits structure S that the baselines ignore.* M is a story, and a story is free. The work of this stage is to make M **risk something** — to derive from it a concrete prediction P about an observable quantity, then go measure that quantity and see whether P lands.

The discipline lives in one constraint, and the whole stage collapses without it: **P must not be the main result, and must not be a mathematical function or near-relative of the main metric.** Two failures to avoid, and they are the same failure wearing two faces.

- **P is the win itself.** "My method gets a lower optimality gap, therefore it must be exploiting structure S." This is circular. The lower gap is *exactly what M was introduced to explain* — citing it back as evidence for M is asking the conclusion to prove its own premise. Every wrong explanation that happens to predict the same win passes this "test", so it discriminates nothing.
- **P is a math relative of the main metric.** Suppose the main metric is runtime and you "probe the mechanism" by measuring iterations-to-convergence — but in your method runtime is iterations times a near-constant per-iteration cost. Then iterations and runtime are one measurement read two ways; probe and main result share a single source, and two apparent pieces of evidence collapse into one. You have not added an independent fact, you have rephrased the existing one. The probed quantity must be **causally downstream of the mechanism and not algebraically tied to the headline** — a genuinely separate observation that M constrains.

A good P is a bet M could lose while the main table still wins. If you cannot state a measurement that an alternative explanation of the same win would get *wrong*, you have not yet derived a probe — you have restated the claim. **This is the dividing line between a publishable and a strong paper:** the table earns publication; the probe that risked M and survived earns belief.

---

## The four probe types, in increasing strength

Draw from these four. They are ordered by how much an alternative explanation has to twist to survive each one. Stand the explanation on **at least two** of them (the `two-probes-minimum` gate) — two independent predictions landing is a different order of evidence from one, because the conjunction of two surviving bets is far harder for a wrong M to fake than either alone.

### 1. Dose-response — sweep the structure, watch the advantage track it

If M says the advantage comes from exploiting structure S, then **the size of the advantage should track the amount of S present.** Use the synthetic generator's knob — the one the experiment-design step built precisely for this — to sweep S from zero to strong, holding everything else fixed, and measure the advantage at each level. M predicts a specific shape: the advantage should rise **monotonically** with S and **vanish at S = 0**. At zero structure there is nothing to exploit, so a method whose only edge is exploiting S must fall back to parity with the baselines there.

Why this is an order of magnitude more evidence than the single-point comparison in the main table: **a single difference has a thousand explanations** — better tuning, a luckier initialization, an implementation detail, a baseline left slightly crippled. A clean monotone curve across, say, eight levels of S leaves very few. For any alternative explanation to produce a curve that rises with S and hits zero advantage at S = 0, it has to *also* be coupled to S in the same graded way — and most spurious causes are not. The curve does not just show *that* you win; it shows you win **in proportion to the thing your story says you exploit.** (Generic illustration: a learned scheduler whose claimed edge is exploiting periodicity in arrivals — sweep the generator from i.i.d. arrivals to strongly periodic ones; M predicts the advantage climbs with the periodicity strength and is zero at i.i.d.) This is the most direct payoff of having built a knobbed generator instead of using only fixed benchmarks.

### 2. Counterfactual destruction — break the structure in the data, not the method

This is the **dual of component ablation**, and a grade above it. Component ablation breaks the *method* (remove a piece, see the advantage shrink). Counterfactual destruction leaves the **method completely untouched** and instead **destroys the structure S in the data**: shuffle the time order so temporal dependence is gone, permute a feature across instances so its correlation with the target is broken, flatten a correlation the method was supposed to be using. M predicts the advantage **disappears** under destruction, because you have removed the very thing it feeds on while changing nothing about the method.

Why this is higher-grade than component ablation: an ablation result is always dilutable by the escape hatch *"the complex component does *something* else"* — when removing a piece hurts, you have shown the piece matters, but not *why*, and a skeptic can attribute the drop to some incidental function of that piece rather than to S. Data-side destruction closes that hatch. The method is byte-for-byte identical before and after; the *only* thing that changed is whether S is present in the input. So a vanished advantage points **precisely** at "you depend on THIS structure" with no room for "the component does something else" — because no component changed. It isolates the dependency on S itself, not on a hunk of code that happens to correlate with S. (Illustration: a method claiming to exploit a correlation between two features — refit and re-evaluate it on a copy of the dataset where one feature is permuted across rows; M predicts the edge collapses to baseline.)

### 3. Intermediate-quantity — open the black box and check an internal claim

The first two probes treat the method as a box and manipulate its input. This one **opens the box**: M usually implies something about an *internal* quantity — a state the method computes on the way to its output — and you check that the internal quantity matches an **external ground truth M predicts**, ground truth you have because the instance is synthetic or analytically understood.

The forms this takes (generic): a prediction module inside the method should have an **error structure that looks the way M says** (e.g. errors concentrated on exactly the hard sub-cases M names, not spread uniformly); learned **embeddings** should **separate the classes M claims they encode** (cluster by the latent structure M says the method discovers); learned **dual variables** in an optimization method should fall in an **economically interpretable range** M predicts (e.g. shadow prices tracking the true marginal value of the binding resource). The internal quantity is a fact about the method's machinery that M makes a sharp claim about, and you go read it directly.

**The technical trap, restated because it is easiest to fall into here:** the probed internal quantity must **not be a mathematical relative of the main metric.** It is tempting, having opened the box, to read out something that is really just the objective re-expressed — and then probe and headline are one source again, two readings of the same number. The internal quantity must be a *genuinely separate* observable that M constrains, not an algebraic shadow of the score you already report. If your "intermediate quantity" can be computed from the main metric without re-running anything, it is not an independent probe.

### 4. Transfer prediction — the strongest, and it must be tested both ways

M is a claim about *what your method exploits*, so it implies a **two-way prediction about transfer**: the method should be **effective on ANOTHER problem that shares structure S** even though it differs in surface form, **and** it should **fall back to mediocre on a problem where S is provably absent.** A correct mechanism is a claim about the world that reaches beyond the benchmark it was tuned on; transfer tests whether that reach is real.

**Test BOTH directions.** Testing only the positive half — "it also works over here, so the mechanism generalizes" — is confirmation bias wearing a generalization costume: you have shown the method works in two places, which a dozen explanations permit. The discriminating half is the **negative** one: M says *where it should fail*, you go to that regime, and you **watch your own method fall back to mediocre.** Predicting your own failure and then seeing it land is **the highest-grade mechanism evidence there is.** It has a Popperian flavor — a theory that predicts its own failure conditions has empirical content, while a theory that predicts only successes (and would be unembarrassed by any outcome) has almost none. When you can say "M implies we are mediocre on problems without S; here is such a problem; here we are, mediocre," there is very little room left for an alternative explanation, because alternatives that explain your wins do not generally also predict your losses in the exact regime M names. (Illustration: a method whose edge is claimed to be low-treewidth graph structure — transfer it to another domain whose instances are also low-treewidth, predict it carries; then transfer it to dense expander-like instances where treewidth is high, predict it falls to baseline — and test that second prediction, not just the first.)

## Presentation — where the probes live and what they earn

The probes usually form an **independent subsection** of the paper — titled something like "Why does it work" or folded into the analysis section — with **one paragraph plus one figure each.** A dose-response curve, a before/after-destruction bar pair, an embedding plot with the predicted separation, a transfer table with both halves. Kept this tight, they are the **direct source** of the review lines every author wants — *"thorough evaluation", "insightful analysis", "the authors clearly understand their method"* — and they are very often **what rescues a borderline paper**, turning a "the method works but so what" reviewer into a champion.

**When a probe misses, the rule is absolute: a probe that misses while the main table still wins means the method works but the STORY is wrong — fix the story, never bury the miss.** A missed probe is not a failed experiment to delete; it is information that M is wrong, and M being wrong while the method still wins means the *real* mechanism is something else you have not yet named. Chase the miss to the corrected explanation. Burying it is the same forking-garden dishonesty the suite exists to prevent, and worse here because the buried miss is *exactly the evidence* that your stated mechanism is fiction — a reviewer or replicator who reruns the probe will find what you hid. Every missed probe earns a logged hypothesis in the 历程 (the append-only lab notebook), the same as any anomalous cell.

## STAGE 4 — The failure boundary: find where you lose, first

The mechanism stage proves *why* you win; the boundary stage finds *where you stop.* These are the same investigation from two ends, and the transfer probe's negative half already did most of the boundary work — one experiment, two uses.

**Actively hunt the slices where the method LOSES.** Not "report the average and hope no one slices it" — go looking, on purpose, for the instance class, the parameter regime, the scale tier where your advantage shrinks to zero or reverses. Then write the boundary as a **conditional statement**, not a hedge: *"when constraint-tightness exceeds X, the advantage disappears"*, *"on instances above N variables the method falls back to baseline", "for correlation below ρ the edge vanishes."* A condition with a number is a finding; "results may vary" is an apology. Put the conditional in the paper's **limitations** section.

**This is pre-emption, not weakness — and the framing matters because the instinct is to hide it.** A reviewer **will** hunt for where your method loses; that is the job. The only question is whether *you* found it first or *they* did. Finding it first inverts the entire dynamic: the same fact that, discovered by a reviewer, reads as "the authors oversold a method that doesn't generalize" reads — when you state it yourself, as a clean condition, in limitations — as **"the authors understand their method deeply."** It is the difference between a desk-reject and a champion. You are not conceding a flaw; you are demonstrating command of your method's operating range.

**Best of all, connect the boundary to the mechanism.** The strongest form is when the losing regime is **exactly where the mechanism's assumption is violated** — the method depends on structure S, and the boundary is precisely the regime where S is absent or too weak to exploit. Then the failure is not a mysterious limitation tacked on at the end; it is a **prediction of M**, confirmed. *"The method exploits S; therefore it must fail where S is absent; here is that regime; here is the failure."* This is why **the transfer probe's negative half IS the boundary**: the experiment that predicts your own mediocrity where S is gone is simultaneously your strongest mechanism evidence *and* your characterized failure condition. One experiment does both jobs — it proves the mechanism by predicting a failure, and it draws the boundary by locating that failure. Run it once; cite it in the discussion as mechanism evidence and in the limitations as the operating boundary.

A boundary that is just "we don't do well on hard instances" is a weakness. A boundary that reads "our advantage requires structure S, vanishes when S is absent (constraint-tightness > X), exactly as the mechanism predicts" is **strength**: it shows you know not just *that* the method works but the precise shape of *when*, and it ties the limitation back to the same story that explains the win. That is what converts a potential desk-reject into evidence of depth.

## How these two stages feed the rest of the pipeline

The probes and the boundary are the paper's **discussion section, written before the prose** — the dose-response figure, the destruction bars, the transfer table with both halves, the boundary stated as a condition. They take their structure from upstream: the **dose-response** probe consumes the knobbed synthetic generator the experiment-design step built; the **counterfactual-destruction** probe reuses the same generator to produce structure-stripped copies of the data; the **intermediate-quantity** probe reads internals against the analytic ground truth a synthetic instance affords. They feed **STAGE 5 (verdict)**: a probe that lands *upgrades* a claim from "supported by the table" to "supported by the table and its mechanism", while a probe that misses forces the claim's mechanism wording to be **revised** — the honest downgrade, not a buried result. And they obey the same firewall the whole suite runs on: a mechanism you derive *after* seeing the confirmation data, and then "validate" on that same data, is a story dredged from noise — the probe's prediction must be derived from M and stated *before* its measurement, with the 历程's timestamps making "did I predict this before or after I looked" an auditable fact rather than a memory. The probe does not discover the mechanism in the data; it tests a mechanism you committed to against an observation you had not yet made.
